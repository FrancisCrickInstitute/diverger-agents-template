Title: Benchmarking skeletonization metrics across vascular network types for permeability estimation

Abstract:
High-resolution imaging such as synchrotron CT (sCT) and Hierarchical Phase-Contrast Tomography (HiP-CT) [1] enables micron-scale resolution of vascular structures, requiring segmentation and skeletonization algorithms to extract the associated architectures. Skeletonization performance varies strongly with network morphology [2]. Tree-like, mesh-like, collapsed, and disconnected structures each present distinct challenges that affect the accuracy of skeleton-based flow modelling.
Our framework evaluates homotopic thinning, distance-ordered thinning and voxel-scooping skeletonization methods using geometric and topological metrics, including the skeletonization super-metric and radius accuracy, linking differences in skeleton quality to permeability tensor estimation. 
In mesh-like structures, algorithms such as TEASAR fail to preserve loops, leading to topological discontinuity [3]. Vessel radii measurements also vary substantially between algorithms, altering predicted vascular network resistance and permeability [2]. Algorithms also differ in preserving connectivity, producing differences in network structure. Both radius and connectivity variations propagate into flow modelling predictions, where accurate representation of paths and resistances is essential.
By comparing skeletonization outputs across diverse morphologies in cardiac imaging datasets, we highlight how algorithmic biases influence permeability estimation, though the absence of a gold standard remains a key challenge. This work informs selection and design of skeletonization algorithms suited to different vascular structures, and supports robust permeability modelling in noisy, artefact-prone or incomplete 3D vascular segmentations.
