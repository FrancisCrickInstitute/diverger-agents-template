Title: Exploiting time to phenotype cell morphology

Abstract:
Cellular processes observed in single cells are frequently dynamic, heterogeneous and spatially distributed, making time-lapse microscopy indispensable for resolving such phenomena. However, characterising complex phenotypes and cell morphologies from image data is nontrivial and, in many cases, this has motivated application-specific image processing methods that are prone to subjectivity and bias. Here we present an objective and general method to assess phenotypic distance: Tracking Objects by Deep Distances to Learn Embedding Representations (TODDLER). Our TODDLER method treats object tracking as a pre-text task to train deep networks that ignore short-time change, like subtle shifts in focus or small movements of cellular organelles, but that can distinguish mid- to long-time change such as cell-cycle stage or cell-type specialisation. The latent representations of these trained networks offer rich measures of morphology and we demonstrate their utility by using our TODDLER method to qualify and quantify cell-cycle stage and cell age in budding yeast.
